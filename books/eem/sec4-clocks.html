    Section 4.5 Clocks and modular arithmetic

    Natural numbers let us count up infinitely forever. Integers extend that to let us count infinitely in two directions. But there are many important phenomena in our lives which are cyclic, not stretching forth forever. You might count forward days of the week, and when you reach the end of the week you start over again at the beginning. Or you might count forward hours, resetting back to \(1\) after you pass \(12\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Example 4.5.1.

    If it is currently \(7\) what time will the clock show after \(8\) hours? One way to calculate this is to count up from \(7\text{,}\) resetting to \(1\) after passing \(12\text{.}\) This is the same as calculating \(7 + 8 = 15\) then subtracting \(12\text{.}\) Thus we see the time will be \(3\text{.}\)
    🔗
    The counting up method is less practical if you ask what time the clock will show after \(73\) hours. What you want to do is, calculate \(7 + 73 = 80\) and get rid of the extra copies of \(12\text{.}\) That is, you want to know what the remainder is if you divide \(80\) by \(12\text{.}\) Doing division with remainder, you can see that \(80 = 6 \cdot 12 + 8\text{,}\) and so the time will be \(8\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    A minor inconvenience with the remainder method is what happens at \(12\text{.}\) For example, if it is \(7\) then in \(5\) hours it will be \(7 + 5 = 12\text{.}\) But if we did the remainder method we’d get a remainder of \(0\) (because \(12\) is a multiple of itself). That is, using this method you have to know that a remainder of \(0\) means the time is \(12\text{,}\) because we don’t allow \(0\) as a time.
    🔗
    This inconvenience goes away if you allow \(0\) as a time. This is commonly done with \(24\) hour clocks. In this system, \(0\) is midnight and the hours count up throughout the day, until \(23:59\) is the minute before the next midnight. Whereas with \(12\) hour clocks each morning and evening is \(12\) hours and we use am versus pm to distinguish them.
    🔗
    The same methods for counting time with \(12\) hour clocks work with \(24\) hour clocks.
    🔗

    Checkpoint 4.5.2.

    Using a \(24\) hour clock, suppose it is \(17\text{.}\) What time will it be after \(11\) hours? After \(71\) hours?
    🔗
    Solution.
    For the first question, we calculate \(17 + 11 = 28\) and then calculate the remainder when we divide by \(24\text{.}\) In this case, that is mostly easily seen by calculating \(28 - 24 = 4\text{,}\) so it will be \(4\text{.}\)
    🔗
    For the second question, we calculate \(17 + 71 = 88\) and then calculate the remainder when we divide by \(24\text{.}\) A little work tells us \(88 = 3 \cdot 24 + 16\text{,}\) and so it will be \(16\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    In Chapter 3 we introduced the mod notation to talk about remainders after division, and this is a place where it is useful. As a reminder, \(a \bmod b\) refers to the remainder when \(a\) is divided by \(b\text{.}\) For instance, we could succintly write our previous calculation as \((17 + 71) \bmod 24 = 16\text{.}\)
    🔗
    If the same process works for both \(12\) hour and \(24\) hour clocks, you might suspect it also works for \(13\) hour clocks, or \(67\) hour clocks, or any number of hours. You would be correct. Indeed, you can do this clock arithmetic not only with addition but also with subtraction and multiplication. This gives what mathematicians call modular arithmetic.
    🔗

    Definition 4.5.3. Modular arithmetic.

    Fix a positive integer \(n\text{.}\) Then arithmetic modulo \(n\) is the following number system.
    • The numbers are the integers \(0, 1, \ldots, n-1\text{.}\) That is, the numbers in arithmetic modulo \(n\) are the natural numbers \(\lt n\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • All arithmetic operations are done \(\bmod n\text{,}\) so that the result is between \(0\) and \(n - 1\text{.}\) You add and take the sum \(\bmod n\text{,}\) subtract and take the difference \(\bmod n\text{,}\) multiply and take the product \(\bmod n\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    To differentiate modular arithmetic from ordinary arithmetic, we write e.g.
    \begin{equation*} 7 \cdot 4 + 1 \equiv 9 \pmod{10}. \end{equation*}
    The use of \(\equiv\) instead of \(=\) reminds us that \(7 + 4 \cdot 1 = 29\) and \(9\) aren’t equal as integers, but are only the same modulo \(10\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    A \(12\) hour clock is the same as arithmetic modulo \(12\) (except that you have to keep in mind we say \(12\) where mathematicians would say \(0\)) and a \(24\) hour clock is the same as arithmetic modulo \(24\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Example 4.5.4.

    Arithmetic modulo \(2\) is the arithmetic of parity—even versus odd. Even means \(0\) modulo \(2\) and odd means \(1\) modulo \(2\text{.}\) You can turn questions about parity into calculations modulo \(2\text{.}\) For example, if you want to know the parity of the product of an even and odd number you can compute \(0 \cdot 1 \equiv 0 \pmod 2\) and conclude that the product must be even.
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 4.5.5.

    If you add the product and sum of two odd numbers, is the result even or odd?
    🔗
    Solution.
    Do arithmetic mod \(2\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} 1\cdot 1 + (1 + 1) \equiv 3 \equiv 1 \pmod 2, \end{equation*}
    so the result must be odd.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 4.5.6.

    Arithmetic modulo \(1\) is boring. The only number is \(0\) and the result of any calculation is that one number \(0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    A nice fact about modular arithmetic is that all the usual algebraic rules for integers also apply. So you can do all the same calculations with all the same rules, just remembering that you have do a mod at the end.
    🔗

    Aside
    There is an exception here, namely that order doesn’t make sense in modular arithmetic. In the integers, it makes sense to say that \(5 \lt 7\text{.}\) But this doesn’t make sense for the integers modulo \(n\text{.}\) Think in terms of clocks: \(5\) is before \(7\text{,}\) but it’s also after \(7\) (the \(7\) from the previous cycle). You can say that \(5\) this morning is before \(7\) this morning, but you can’t in general say \(5\) is before \(7\text{.}\)
    🔗
    But so long as you just work with the arithmetic operations you can freely use the familiar rules.
    🔗

    Theorem 4.5.7.

    The integers modulo \(n\) form a commutative ring. That is, they satisfy all the axioms in Theorem 4.2.6.
    🔗
    🔗
    One use of modular arithmetic is in cryptography, the practice of encrypting data to ensure secure communication without eavesdropping. In brief, the way it works is your data, such as bank info, is turned into a number. Some calculations are done modulo a really big \(n\) with this number and a secret key. This produces a ciphertext, which is then transmitted over the internet. Knowing the secret key you can reverse the calculations to recover the data. But without that knowledge it’s very difficult and slow to reverse the calculations. So someone spying on your internet traffic won’t be able to figure out your bank info.
    🔗
