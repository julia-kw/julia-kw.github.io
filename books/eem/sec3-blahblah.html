    Section 3.2 Divisibility Rules

    In this section we learn rules for checking divisibility by a small number. Let’s start with the easiest and, for that reason, the least useful.
    🔗

    Theorem 3.2.1. Rule for divisibility by \(1\).

    A natural number \(n\) is always divisible by \(1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.
    Because \(n = 1 \cdot n\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Let’s see a more useful rule.
    🔗

    Theorem 3.2.2. Rule for divisibility by \(10\).

    A natural number \(n\) is divisible by \(10\) if the last digit of \(n\) is \(0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.
    Every other digit of \(n\) represents \(10\) times something, so the only way \(n\) could fail to be a multiple of \(10\) is if the units digit is nonzero.
    🔗
    🔗
    Knowing the rule for divisibility by \(10\) lets us also know rules for divisibility by \(2\) and \(5\text{,}\) because \(10 = 2 \cdot 5\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Theorem 3.2.3. Rule for divisibility by \(2\).

    A natural number \(n\) is divisible by \(2\) if and only if its last digit is even: \(0, 2, 4, 6,\) or \(8\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.
    The other digits of \(n\) represent a number divisible by \(10\text{,}\) and hence also divisible by \(2\text{.}\) So all that remains is to check that the quantity represented by the units digit is divisible by \(2\text{.}\) Those are the five even numbers which are one digit.
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 3.2.4. Rule for divisibility by \(5\).

    A natural number \(n\) is divisible by \(5\) if and only if its last digit is either \(0\) or \(5\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.
    Same logic as the rule for \(2\text{.}\) The only possible issue is the units digit, so check if the units digit is one of the two one-digit numbers divisible by \(5\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Before we see the rule for \(3\text{,}\) let’s look at an example. Consider the number \(123\text{.}\) You can break this apart as follows.
    \begin{align*} 123 \amp = 1 \cdot 10^2 + 2 \cdot 10 + 3 \cdot 1\\ \amp = 1 \cdot (9 + 1)^2 + 2 \cdot (9 + 1) + 3 \cdot 1 \end{align*}
    When you expand out the multiplications, everything with a \(9\) as a term is a multiple of \(3\text{.}\) The remainder are \(1 \cdot 1\text{,}\) \(2 \cdot 1\text{,}\) and \(3 \cdot 1\text{.}\) So \(123\) is a multiple of \(3\) if and only if these remainders sum to a multiple of \(3\text{.}\) But this is just looking at the sum of the digits.
    🔗

    Theorem 3.2.5. Rule for divisibility by \(3\).

    A natural number \(n\) is divisible by \(3\) if and only if the sum of its digits is a multiple of \(3\text{.}\) (To check if the sum of the digits is a multiple of \(3\text{,}\) repeat this process until you get to a small enough number you don’t need to check.)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.
    Do what we did with \(123\text{,}\) except you have to write it abstractly to work for any number.
    🔗
    🔗
    The same idea gives us a divisibility rule for \(9\text{.}\) What we used in the breakdown for \(3\) was \(9\)s, and those will also give divisibility by \(9\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Theorem 3.2.6. Rule for divisibility by \(9\).

    A natural number \(n\) is divisible by \(9\) if and only if the sum of its digits is a multiple of \(9\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.
    Same as with \(3\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    We can get a rule for \(6\) by combining the rules for \(2\) and \(3\text{.}\) This is because \(6 = 2 \cdot 3\) and so checking divisibility by \(6\) is the same as checking divisibility by both \(2\) and \(3\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Theorem 3.2.7. Rule for divisibility by \(6\).

    A natural number \(n\) is divisible by \(6\) if and only if its last digit is even and the sum of its digits is a multiple of \(3\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.
    Explained above.
    🔗
    🔗
    The rules for \(4\) and \(8\) are maybe a little less convenient, but they are the best we can do.
    🔗

    Theorem 3.2.8. Rule for divisibility by \(4\).

    A natural number \(n\) is divisible by \(4\) if and only if its last two digits form a number divisible by \(4\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.
    The rest of the digits represent a quantity divisible by \(100\text{,}\) and \(100\) is a multiple of \(4\text{.}\) So the only thing to check is that the last two digits are divisible by \(4\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 3.2.9. Rule for divisibility by \(8\).

    A natural number \(n\) is divisible by \(8\) if and only if its last three digits form a number divisible by \(8\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.
    Similar to the rule for \(4\text{.}\) Namely, the rest of the digits represent a quantity divisible by \(1000\text{,}\) and \(1000 = 8 \cdot 125\text{.}\) So the only thing to check is that the last three digits are divisible by \(8\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    There’s too many two digit and three digit numbers to memorize which of them are divisible by \(4\) and \(8\text{.}\) But at least checking a two or three digit number is easier than many digit number.
    🔗
    Conspicuously missing from this list is a divisibility rule for \(7\text{.}\) This is not because there is no rule for \(7\text{,}\) but because any rule for \(7\) is too complicated to be practically useful. You will discover this in an exercise for this chapter.
    🔗
    Putting these rules together, we have a method to get the prime factorization of a number \(n\text{,}\) provided its only prime factors are small. Namely, run through the rules. If you determine \(n\) is divisible by, say, \(5\text{,}\) then divide it by \(5\text{.}\) Keep going with this process until all the rules fail. At that point, you may have to manually check if you can divide by small primes like \(7\) or \(11\text{,}\) until you recognize that what’s left cannot be factored any further.
    🔗

    Checkpoint 3.2.10.

    Find the prime factorization of \(1512\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Answer.
    \(1512 = 2^3 \cdot 3^3 \cdot 7.\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Solution.
    Immediately you can see that \(1512\) is divisible by \(2\text{.}\) You might further notice that it’s divisible by \(4\text{,}\) because \(12 = 4 \cdot 3\text{.}\) Divide out by \(4 = 2^2\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} 1512 = 2^2 \cdot 378. \end{equation*}
    This number is even so we can pull out another \(2\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} 1512 = 2^3 \cdot 189. \end{equation*}
    We now see we cannot factor out any more \(2\)s. Next we check divisibility by \(3\text{:}\) \(1 + 8 + 9 = 18\text{,}\) and repeating the summing the digits rule we get \(1 + 8 = 9\text{.}\) This is a multiple of \(9\text{,}\) so in fact we can pull out two copies of \(3\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} 1512 = 2^3 \cdot 3^2 \cdot 21. \end{equation*}
    We repeat the rule for \(3\) with \(21\text{:}\) \(2 + 1 = 3\text{.}\) Thus we can pull out yet another \(3\text{:}\)
    \begin{equation*} 1512 = 2^3 \cdot 3^3 \cdot 7. \end{equation*}
    At this point, all that is left over is \(7\text{,}\) which is small enough that we can recognize it as a prime. We can’t go any further, so we have the final prime factorization.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
