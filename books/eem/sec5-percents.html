    Section 5.4 Percents

    To close out this chapter we look at a commonly used kind of fraction, namely percents. The meaning is in the name: percent = per cent = per hundred (think cent as in centimeter).
    🔗

    Definition 5.4.1. Percents.

    Let \(X\) be a number. Then \(X\%\text{,}\) pronounced \(X\) percent, is the fraction
    \begin{equation*} \frac{X}{100}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Percents are useful when talking about parts of a whole. For instance, when setting up direct deposit for your paycheck you can choose how to split it between multiple accounts. Putting \(100\%\) in one account would be putting the whole paycheck in one place. Or you might instead split it \(30\%\) and \(70\%\) between two accounts; the first account would receive \(\frac{30}{100}\) of your paycheck while the second would receive \(\frac{70}{100}\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Checkpoint 5.4.2.

    What percent represents one quarter? One fifth? One tenth?
    🔗
    🔗
    One way to remember the meaning of the percents is to look at the symbol \(\%\text{.}\) The slash represents a fraction and the two O’s are the zeroes in \(100\text{.}\)
    🔗
    One place you may see percents used to describe relative changes. A population of a city might increase by \(10\%\) over a decade. If the initial population was \(10,000\) people, then the extra \(10\%\) is \(10,000 \cdot \frac{10}{100} = 1000\) people, so the new population is \(11,000\text{.}\) You could equivalently calculate the new population by
    \begin{equation*} 10,000 \cdot 110\% = 10,000 \cdot \frac{110}{100} = 11,000, \end{equation*}
    where the \(100\%\) represents the initial population and the \(10\%\) is the increase.
    🔗
    In the other direction, percents also can describe decreases. Your car might depreciate in value by \(5\%\) in a year. If its initial value was \(20,000\) dollars then its new value is
    \begin{equation*} 20,000 \cdot \frac{95}{100} = 20,000 - 20,000 \cdot \frac{5}{100} = 19,000 \end{equation*}
    dollars. Because the value is decreasing we subtract the \(5\%\) from the \(100\%\) representing the initial value.
    🔗
    There’s nothing special about the number \(100\text{.}\) It’s used for percents because it’s a round number that’s familiar from our base \(10\) number system. You could use the same idea but pick a different number for the denominator. Though less commonly used than percents, some of these have been used enough to have names.
    🔗

    Definition 5.4.3. Permilles.

    Let \(X\) be a number. Then \(X\)‰, pronounced \(X\) permille, is the fraction
    \begin{equation*} \frac{X}{1000}. \end{equation*}
    Think: per mille = per thousand (mille as in milligram).
    🔗
    🔗
    For example, \(100\)‰ is the fraction \(\frac{100}{1000} = \frac{1}{10}\text{.}\) It is equivalent to \(10\%\text{.}\)
    🔗
    You also don’t need to use a power of ten. You could, say, break things up into sixtieths. If this seems weird to you, it’s how we break up hours and minutes. Or you could break things up into \(360\)ths, like how the degree measurement for angles breaks a circle up into \(360\)ths of the whole.
    🔗
    These examples have special names and notation. If you wanted to be talk about it for any denominator, you arrive back at fractions. Indeed, you can use this as a way to think about what fractions are. We divide wholes into parts—per \(100\) for percents, per \(60\) for seconds, and so on. Fractions are a generalization of that idea to divide wholes into any number of parts.
    🔗

    Aside
    The reason why \(60\) and \(360\) get used is that they are highly divisible. Many different integers evenly divide into them. On the other hand, percents have fewer divisors. For instance, you cannot express \(\frac13\) as a whole number fraction. (You can express it using decimals, but even then writing it exactly requires infinitely many digits. We will talk about these in Chapter 6.) On the other hand, percents fit nicely with our base-ten numeral system.
