    Section 6.3 Approximations

    In real life, you never know quantities to exact precision. If your speedometer reads that your car is going \(35\) miles per hour, it could be \(35.4\) miles per hour, or \(29.8\) or \(35.0\text{.}\) Indeed, that level of precision isn’t helpful, so you would just round all of these to \(35\) miles per hour.
    🔗
    A useful feature of writing numbers in decimals is that it makes it straightforward to handle approximations. You can look at, say \(4.12\) and immediately see it is close to \(4\text{.}\) In contrast, it would take a little thought to see that \(\frac{103}{25}\) is close to \(4\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Let’s first talk about rounding to a whole number.
    🔗

    Definition 6.3.1. Rounding to an integer.

    Rounding a real number \(x\) to an integer is a way to replace \(x\) with an integer which is close to \(x\text{.}\) There are few ways you can do this.
    Rounding toward zero, also called truncation, is the closest integer between \(x\) and \(0\text{.}\) If \(x\) is written in decimal, this is deleting all the digits past the decimal point. For instance, the truncation of \(-32.1\) is \(-32\text{.}\)
    🔗
    • The floor of \(x\) is the closest integer \(\le x\text{.}\) This is also called rounding toward \(-\infty\) or rounding down. If \(x\) is positive this is equivalent to truncation.
      🔗
      🔗
    • The ceiling of \(x\) is the closest integer \(\ge x\text{.}\) This is also called rounding toward \(\infty\) or rounding up.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Rounding to the nearest integer replaces \(x\) with the integer closest to \(x\text{.}\) That is, if the first digit of \(x\) is \(0\) through \(4\) then you round toward zero, and if the first digit of \(x\) is \(5\) through \(9\) then you round away from zero.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 6.3.2.

    Consider the number \(\pi \approx 3.14\text{.}\) Rounding toward zero, rounding down, and rounding to the nearest integer all round to \(3\text{.}\) Rounding up rounds to \(4\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Or consider \(x = 3.6\text{.}\) Rounding toward zero and rounding down both round to \(3\text{.}\) Rounding up and rounding to the nearest integer both round to \(4\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 6.3.3.

    With positive numbers, rounding toward zero and rounding down are equivalent. The distinction between them shows up for negative numbers. Consider \(x = -3.4\text{.}\) Rounding down rounds to \(-4\) while rounding toward zero rounds to \(-3\text{.}\) On the other hand, for negative numbers rounding up and rounding toward zero are equivalent.
    🔗
    🔗
    Sometimes people simply say “rounding” to mean rounding to the nearest integer. If you see it said without specifying the method this is probably what they mean.
    🔗

    Aside
    When rounding to the nearest integer, what do you do when a number is exactly halfway between two integers? Do you round \(3.5\) to \(3\) or to \(4\text{?}\) The two integers are tied for how close they are, so how do you decide the tie-breaker?
    🔗
    The rule given above is to always round up in case of a tie. For some purposes, however, this rule can introduce bias. To illustrate this, imagine you are using a digital scale which gives weights accurate to one tenth of the gram and you are rounding to the nearest gram. For instance, it might display a weight of \(23.4\) grams, which you would round to \(23\) grams. One possible digit after the decimal point, namely \(0\text{,}\) doesn’t give you any error when you round. Of the remaining nine, eight of them are balanced. Rounding down from \(23.1\) introduces an error of \(0.1\) below the measured weight, but rounding up from \(23.9\) introduces an error of \(0.1\) above the measured weight. If you do lots of measurements on average these errors will cancel. The remaining digit, \(5\text{,}\) always rounds upward, introducing an error of \(0.5\) above the measured weight. This one error is not canceled out, and so if you weight many different things and added them up the errors would tend to bias upward.
    🔗
    This is, admittedly, quite in the weeds. If you’re using your digital scale for, say, home baking then the error is too small to matter. But for more precise matters, such as scientific or engineering uses, this bias can matter. In such a case, you’ll want to use a different tie-breaker rule.
    🔗
    The lesson here is, there is no one rule that’s the best for all circumstances. There are trade-offs of convenience and precision, and what is best depends on what you’re using it for. This is common. Mathematics may furnish many possible methods one could use, and no one of them is better than the others for all applications.
    🔗
    Indeed, this is true for rounding. Is it best to round up? Round down? Round to the nearest integer? The answer is: it depends.
    🔗
    Rounding to an integer is the same as rounding to the units place. You can do the same process, but rounding to any other place. When you round to the units digit, all digits to the right—that is, all digits after the decimal point—become \(0\)s. The same applies when rounding to other places. For example, if you round to the hundreds place then the tens and units digits, as well as all digits after the decimal, become \(0\text{.}\) Or if you round to the tenths place, then all digits after that—hundreths, thousandths, etc.—get set to \(0\text{.}\)
    🔗
    As with rounding to an integer, there are multiple methods to round to the nearest tens, etc. You can round up to the next thousand, you can round to the nearest thousand, you can round toward zero to the next thousand, and so on. If you round to the nearest, you can tell which direction to round by looking at the next place. For example, to round to the nearest tenth you look at the digit in the hundreths place. The digits \(0\) to \(4\) you round toward zero, and the digits \(5\)to \(9\) you round away from zero.
    🔗

    Example 6.3.4.

    Consider the number \(231.78\text{.}\) Rounded to the nearest tenth it is \(231.8\text{.}\) Rounded down to the tenths place it is \(231.7\text{.}\) Rounded to the nearest ten it is \(230\text{.}\) Rounded up to the next ten it is \(240\text{.}\) Rounded to the nearest thousand it is \(0\) while rounded up to the next thousand it is \(1000\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Physicists tell us that the speed of light is \(299,792,458\) meters per second. This value is known exactly. But when doing calculations, you probably don’t need the number to that precision. It’s enough to say it’s about three hundred million meters per second. Rounding gives you an easier number to say and work with.
    🔗

    Aside
    The reason we know the speed of light exactly is because that is the modern definition of the meter: it is defined to be \(\frac{1}{299,792,458}\) of the distance traveled by light in one second. This is not the original definition of the meter. Older definitions were based on distances around the globe, so as to create a standard that could be used internationally. Advances in physics allowed this to be replaced by the current, light-based definition.
    🔗
    Most quantities in the real world aren’t known to that level of precision. For example, suppose you measure the sides of a square box to be about \(1\) foot. Using the Pythagorean theorem, you calculate that the length of the diagonal of the box is \(\sqrt 2 = 1.414213562\ldots\) feet. It wouldn’t make sense to report that length to that level of precision. The sides of the box are not exactly \(1\) foot, and so the diagonal is not exactly \(\sqrt 2\) feet. For example, if the true length of the side was \(1.05\) feet then the diagonal would be about \(1.48\) feet long. For this reason, it makes sense to round the value; reporting too precise a measure gives the misimpression that you know the value to that many decimal points.
    🔗
    Significant digits are a convenient way to organize this information. The idea is, the number of digits you write down represents how precisely you know a value. These are the digits that are significant, and any other digits are noise and shouldn’t be reported. When doing calculations, you round your final values to the same number of significant digits. You can use the following rules to read and use significant digits.
    • Nonzero digits within the level of precision are always significant. You should round so digits outside the level of precision aren’t reported. For example, if you have two digits of precision then you should report \(1.34\) as \(1.3\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • Zeroes left of the first nonzero digit are never significant.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Zeroes after the decimal point are significant and should be reported if they are within the level of precision. For example, if you have three digits of precision then you should write \(23.0\) instead of \(23\text{,}\) to communicate that the first digit past the decimal point is signicant.
      🔗
      🔗
    • Zeroes before the decimal point may or may not be significant. For example, \(4200\) might represent a value with two digits of precision, or it might represent one with three or four digits of precision. Some underline or overline the last significant zero to indicate the level of precision. For instance, one could write \(42\underline{0}0\) to represent three digits of precision. But this is not a universal convention.
      🔗
      🔗
    • When doing calculations with multiple numbers, values should use the smallest number of significant digits from your inputs.
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗

    Example 6.3.5.

    You have two strips of cloth, one \(420\) centimeters long and the other \(370\) centimeters long. (For both of these, the numbers are at two digits of precision.) If you need \(2\) centimeters of length from each to sew them together, how long will your sewn-together strips be?
    🔗
    To answer this, calculate
    \begin{equation*} 420 + 670 - 2\cdot 2 = 786. \end{equation*}
    You then round to two digits of precision, giving you \(790\) centimeters. Note that this is the same answer you’d get if you just calculated \(420 + 670\) and ignored the amount of cloth needed for the stitching.
    🔗
    If instead the cloths were \(42\) and \(37\) centimeters long, with the same length needed for sewing, you would calculate
    \begin{equation*} 42 + 37 - 2\cdot 2 = 75. \end{equation*}
    This is already at the correct level of precision so you don’t need to round to report the total length will be \(75\) centimeters. Note that this is not the same value as \(42 + 37 = 79\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Intuitively, it should make sense that if the strips are really long then the length needed for sewing them together is a rounding error. Whereas if the lengths of the cloths are close to the amount reserved to sew them together then that difference is significant.
    🔗

    Checkpoint 6.3.6.

    Suppose you measure that a \(2 \times 4\) toy building brick is \(31.8\) milimeters long. If you placed \(100\) of them in a row, with no space between them, how long would it be? What if you did \(23\) of them
    🔗
    Solution.
    The calculations are each a single multiplication. For the first, \(31.8 \cdot 100 = 3180\) milimeters. This is already the correct level of precision—three digits, matching the input—so you don’t have to round. For the second, \(31.8 \cdot 23 = 731.4\text{.}\) This is more digits than your level of precision, so you should round to \(731\) milimeters.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    Checkpoint 6.3.7.

    You have a rope which you measured to be \(8.0\) meters long. You cut off \(1.25\) meters of rope from it. How much length is left over?
    🔗
    Solution.
    Calculate
    \begin{equation*} 8.0 - 1.25 = 6.75. \end{equation*}
    This is three digits of precision, whereas your input measurements had two and three digits of precision. You should round to the smaller of those, so round to two digits of precision: you have \(6.8\) meters of rope left over.
    🔗
    The rounding here represents the uncertainty in your initial measurements. You measured that the rope was originally \(8.0\) meters, but it could be \(8.00\) or \(8.05\) or \(8.09\text{.}\) You don’t know, and these will give slightly different lengths for what’s left after cutting. You can’t be more sure of your calculated length then you are of the measurements that went into it.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    A practical way of dealing with both significant figures and magnitudes—is the number approximately one million? one trillion? one thousandth?—is given by scientific notation. Let me give an example first. The speed of light is, to four digits of precision, \(299,800,000\) meters per second. The magnitude of this number is some number of hundred millions, or \(10^8\)s. How many \(10^8\)s is it? The first significant digit gives the whole number, and the rest are the fractional part. Namely, this is \(2.998\) many \(10^8\)s. We represent this succinctly as a multiplication: the speed of light is approximately \(2.998 \times 10^8\) meters per second.
    🔗

    Definition 6.3.8. Scientific notation.

    A number is written in scientific notation when it is written in the form
    \begin{equation*} D.d_1 d_2\cdots d_n \times 10^k, \end{equation*}
    where \(D, d_1, d_2, \ldots, d_n\) are digits \(0\)–\(9\) and \(k\) is an integer. The number \(k\) is called the exponent and the digits are called the significand. Phrased another way, a number is written in scientific notation when it is written in the form
    \begin{equation*} s \times 10^k\text{,} \end{equation*}
    where \(s\) is a number between \(1\) and \(10\) and \(k\) is an integer.
    🔗
    🔗

    Aside
    Some calculators use a shorthand for scientific notation, writing \(2.43E5\) to mean \(2.43 \times 10^{5}\text{.}\) (The E naturally stands for “Exponent”.)
    🔗

    Example 6.3.9.

    • You write \(12.3\) in scientific notation as \(1.23 \times 10^1\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • You write \(4.789\) in scientific notation as \(4.789 \times 10^0\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • You write \(0.086\) in scientific notation as \(8.6 \times 10^{-2}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • You write \(800,800\) in scientific notation as \(8.008 \times 10^5\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • The number \(345 \times 10^{100}\) isn’t quite in scientific notation. To write it that way, it should be \(3.45 \times 10^{102}\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    The virtue of scientific notation is that it lets you quickly see how large a quantity is and its number of significant digits. It is commonly used, as the name suggests, in science. For example, in chemistry Avogadro’s constant is used to convert between mass of a chemical and the number of particles therein. It is approximately \(6.022 \times 10^{23}\text{.}\) This is too large in magnitude to comfortably write out in the Hindu–Arabic numeral system:
    \begin{equation*} 6022000000000000000000000 \end{equation*}
    (This number is actually wrong; it’s \(6.022 \times 10^{24}\) so it’s off by an order of magnitude. But checking that requires counting digits and is prone to mistakes!)
    🔗
    Another way this problem is handled is with prefixes for units in the metric system. Rather than write \(6000\) grams one writes \(6\) kilograms. Or instead of saying that visible light has a wavelength of about \(5 \times 10^{-7}\) meters you can say it’s about \(500\) nanometers.
