    Section 3.3 GCD and LCM

    Let’s talk about some other topics regarding divisibility. Given two integers, they will have some divisors in common. For example, consider \(24\) and \(30\text{.}\) Their common divisors are \(1, 2, 3,\) and \(6\text{.}\) It can be useful to know about common divisors. This is used, for instance, in fraction arithmetic; see Chapter 5.
    🔗

    Definition 3.3.1. Greatest common divisor (GCD).

    Given two numbers \(a,b\text{,}\) their greatest common divisor (GCD) is the largest number which divides both of them. It also makes sense to talk about the GCD of a list of more than two numbers; it is the largest number which divides all of them. We write \(\gcd(a,b)\) for the GCD of \(a\) and \(b\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Note that \(\gcd(a,0)\) is always \(a\text{.}\) This is because any number divides \(0\)—after all, \(0 \cdot n = 0\) for any \(n\)—and so the largest common divisor is the largest divisor of \(a\text{,}\) namely \(a\) itself. On the other hand, \(\gcd(0,0)\) is undefined; every number divides both \(0\) and \(0\text{,}\) so there isn’t a largest one.
    🔗
    One way to determine the GCD of a group of numbers is to write out all of their prime factorizations, look for the primes common to all, and then multiply them together.
    🔗

    Checkpoint 3.3.2.

    Find the GCD of \(168\) and \(180\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    First find the prime factorizations, by the methods of the last section.
    \begin{align*} 168 \amp= 2^3 \cdot 3 \cdot 7 \\ 180 \amp= 2^2 \cdot 3^2 \cdot 5 \end{align*}
    Looking at these factorizations, we see that they have in common \(2^2\) and \(3\text{.}\) We multiply those together to get \(\gcd(168,180) = 12\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    This method works, but it ranges from tedious to practically impossible, because it first requires you to find the prime factorizations of all the numbers. This is reasonable for two digit numbers, but it quickly gets awful. Surely, one hopes, there is a better way. If you are like me, then in elementary school you were not taught a better way. This happened because math teachers hate their students and want them to suffer. You can break the cycle of violence, however. You can teach your own students to use the Euclidean algorithm.
    🔗

    Theorem 3.3.3.

    If \(a \gt b\) are positive numbers then \(\gcd(a,b) = \gcd(b, a - b)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.
    Suppose \(d\) is a divisor of both \(a\) and \(b\text{.}\) That is, \(a = md\) and \(b = nd\) for some numbers \(m,n\text{.}\) Then \(a - b = md - nd = (m - n) d\text{,}\) so \(d \mid a - b\text{.}\) A similar argument shows that if \(d\) is a divisor of both \(b\) and \(a - b\text{,}\) then \(d \mid a\text{.}\) Together, these show that the common divisors of \(a\) and \(b\) are the same as the common divisors of \(b\) and \(a - b\text{.}\) Since they have the same set of common divisors, they have the same largest common divisor.
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 3.3.4. The Euclidean algorithm for finding the GCD, first version.

    To find the GCD of \(a\) and \(b\text{,}\) where we assume \(a \ge b\text{,}\) follow this process.
    1. If \(a = b\text{,}\) then we are done. Return that value as the GCD.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. If \(a \gt b\text{,}\) then instead compute \(\gcd(b, a - b)\text{,}\) using this same process.
      🔗
      🔗
    While this algorithm may look circular, at each stage you have decreased one of the two numbers you are looking at. You can’t keep decreasing numbers forever—you eventually hit bottom at \(1\)—so this process will eventually stop.
    🔗
    🔗
    An algorithm like this, which reduces a problem to a smaller or easier case until it eventually bottoms out, is called a recursive algorithm.
    🔗

    Checkpoint 3.3.5.

    Find \(\gcd(30,18)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    We recursively apply the Euclidean algorithm (first version).
    \begin{align*} \gcd(30, 18) \amp= \gcd(18, 30 - 18) = \gcd(18,12)\\ \amp= \gcd(12, 18 - 12) = \gcd(12, 6)\\ \amp= \gcd(6, 12 - 6) = \gcd(6,6)\\ \amp= 6 \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    This algorithm is fastest when one number is approximately half as big as the other. When one is much bigger than the other or they are close in size it can take a long time for the subtraction process to bottom out. There is a faster version of the Euclidean algorithm using a different operation from subtraction.
    🔗

    Definition 3.3.6. The mod operation.

    Let \(a\) and \(b\) be natural numbers. Then \(a \bmod b\) is the remainder when \(a\) is divided by \(b\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Mod is short for “modulo”, which is a Latinate word meaning “up to this specified difference”. This use comes from its use in phrases like “\(7\) and \(37\) are equivalent modulo \(10\)”, meaning that \(7\) and \(37\) are equivalent after you divide out by \(10\) and only look at the remainder. A slightly more informal use would be “\(3.5\) and \(8.5\) are the same modulo the whole number part”. If you want your friends to think you’re a nerd, you can sprinkle this word into your everday vocabulary.
    🔗
    For an example, \(39 \bmod 4 = 3\) because \(39 = 4 \cdot 9 + 3\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Aside
    When calculating mods by hand, often the quickest way is to repeatedly subtract until you can see the remainder. For example, to compute \(13 \bmod 7\) you can just do \(13 - 7 = 6\text{.}\) Or to compute \(13 \bmod 4\) you repeatedly subtract \(4\) until you get a result smaller than \(4\text{:}\)
    \begin{align*} 13 - 4 \amp= 9\\ 9 - 4 \amp= 5\\ 5 - 4 \amp= 1, \end{align*}
    so \(13 \bmod 4 = 1\text{.}\)
    🔗
    This method is reasonable when there aren’t many subtractions to do, but is clunky when calculating \(a \bmod b\) for \(a\) much larger than \(b\text{.}\)
    🔗
    If you are using a calculator, a trick to calculate mods is do the division then multiply the fractional part by the divisor. For example, to calculate \(13 \bmod 4\) you’d calculate \(13 / 4 = 3.25\) and then you’d mulitply \(0.25 \times 4 = 1\) to get the remainder. Some calculators and most programming languages will have a mod function to calculate it directly, in which case you can just use that.
    🔗

    Checkpoint 3.3.7.

    Explain why \(a \bmod 10\) is always the units digit of \(a\text{.}\) How do you quickly see \(a \bmod 100\text{?}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 3.3.8.

    If \(a \gt b\) are positive numbers then \(\gcd(a,b) = \gcd(b, a \bmod b)\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Proof.
    The idea is much the same as before. Suppose that \(d\) is a divisor of both \(a\) and \(b\text{.}\) That is, \(a = md\) and \(b = nd\) for some numbers \(m,n\text{.}\) Doing division with remainder, \(a = qb + r\text{,}\) where \(q\) is the quotient and the remainder \(r = a \bmod b\text{.}\) Then,
    \begin{align*} r \amp= a - qb\\ \amp= md - qnd\\ \amp= (m - qn)d, \end{align*}
    showing that \(d \mid r\text{.}\) A similar argument shows that if \(d\) is a divisor of both \(b\) and \(a \bmod b\) then \(d \mid a\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Like before, this shows that the common divisors of \(a\) and \(b\) are the same as the common divisors of \(b\) and \(a \bmod b\text{,}\) and so they have the same greatest common divisor.
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 3.3.9. The Euclidean algorithm for finding the GCD, fast version.

    To find the GCD of \(a\) and \(b\text{,}\) where we assume \(a \ge b\text{,}\) follow this process.
    1. If \(b = 0\text{,}\) then we are done. Return \(a = \gcd(a,0)\) as the GCD.
      🔗
      🔗
    2. If \(a \gt b\text{,}\) then instead compute \(\gcd(b, a \bmod b)\text{,}\) using this same process.
      🔗
      🔗
    Like before, this recursive process eventually bottoms out because we’re dealing with smaller numbers at each step
    🔗
    🔗
    Repeatedly computing \(a \bmod b\) instead of \(a - b\) will give you a much faster decreasing sequence, and so this version of the algorithm will take fewer steps to bottom out. The trade-off is that computing mod is usually slower than computing subtraction. You can use a calculator to help you.
    🔗

    Checkpoint 3.3.10.

    Find the GCD of \(168\) and \(180\text{.}\)
    🔗
    Solution.
    We repeatedly apply the Euclidean algorithm (fast version).
    \begin{align*} \gcd(180, 168) \amp= \gcd(180, 180 \bmod 168) = \gcd(168,12)\\ \amp= \gcd(12, 168 \bmod 12) = \gcd(12, 0)\\ \amp= 12 \end{align*}
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Going in the opposite direction, given two integers you can talk about their common multiples. For instance, the common multiples of \(4\) and \(6\) are
    \begin{equation*} 12, 24, 36, \ldots \end{equation*}
    Just like two numbers have a largest common divisor, they also have a smallest common multiple.
    🔗

    Definition 3.3.11. Least common multiple (LCM) \(\lcm\).

    Given two numbers \(a,b\) their least common multiple, written \(\lcm(a,b)\) is the smallest number which is a multiple of both of them. In symbols, \(\lcm(a,b)\) is the smallest number \(\ell\) so that \(a \mid \ell\) and \(b \mid \ell\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Theorem 3.3.12.

    The least common multiple can be calculated as follows:
    \begin{equation*} \lcm(a,b) = \frac{ab}{\gcd(a,b)}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Example 3.3.13.

    The LCM of \(8\) and \(10\) can be calculated as
    \begin{equation*} \lcm(8,10) = \frac{8 \cdot 10}{\gcd(8,10)} = \frac{80}{2} = 40, \end{equation*}
    where you can use any of the previously described methods for finding the GCD. In this example, the numbers are small enough that you might immediately see that their GCD is \(2\text{.}\) (And if you didn’t immediately see that then you can look at their prime factorizations or use the Euclidean algorithm.)
    🔗
    🔗
    An alternative way to compute the LCM goes through prime factorizations. The smallest common multiple of two numbers must contain the prime factors of both of them, but without anything else. So to find the LCM list out the numbers’ prime factorizations. Then grab all the primes which occur in at least one of the two factorizations, with the largest exponent that occurs.
    🔗

    Example 3.3.14.

    To calculate the LCM of \(84\) and \(120\text{,}\) first find their prime factorizations.
    \begin{align*} 120 \amp= 2^3 \cdot 3 \cdot 5\\ 84 \amp= 2^2 \cdot 3 \cdot 7 \end{align*}
    We now go through the factorizations, grabbing the largest exponent which occurs on one side or the other. Thus we get
    \begin{equation*} \lcm(84,120) = 2^3 \cdot 3 \cdot 5 \cdot 7 = 840. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    This method also can be used to explain why the division by the GCD method works. Namely, in the formula
    \begin{equation*} \lcm(a,b) = \frac{ab}{\gcd(a,b)} \end{equation*}
    the numerator \(ab\) says to take the prime factorization of \(a\) and add in the prime factorization of \(b\text{.}\) But some primes are added in twice, namely the ones that are common to both factorization. So to throw out the excess we divide by the common prime factors, which is the same as dividing by the GCD.
    🔗

    Checkpoint 3.3.15.

    What is \(\lcm(5,0)\text{?}\) What about \(\lcm(a,0)\) for any positive number \(a\text{?}\)
