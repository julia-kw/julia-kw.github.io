    Section 1.6 The arithmetic of natural numbers

    Now that we’ve seen operations for combining sets we can talk about their counterpart operations on numbers.
    🔗
    If you have four mangos and three pineapples, how many fruits do you have in total? If \(M\) is the set of your mangos and \(P\) is the set of your pineapples, what’s being asked is, what’s the cardinality of \(M \cup P\text{?}\)
    🔗

    Definition 1.6.1. Addition of natural numbers.

    Suppose \(m\) and \(n\) are natural numbers. Let \(A\) and \(B\) be disjoint sets with \(\card A = m\) and \(\card B = n\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{equation*} m + n = \card{A \cup B}. \end{equation*}
    When we need to refer to the different parts of addition, we call \(m + n\) the sum, while \(m\) and \(n\) are the summands.
    🔗
    🔗
    You can phrase this as being about counting choices. If you have your choice either from one of \(m\) options or from one of \(n\) options, then you have \(m+n\) many choices. For example, if a restaurant offers \(3\) options of soup or \(2\) options of salad, that is \(3+2\) many options total.
    🔗
    Once you know about addition you can give a clear definition of what it means for one natural number to be bigger than another. On the level of sets, the idea is, a finite set \(A\) is larger than another \(B\) if you can add things to \(B\) to make it the same size as \(A\text{.}\) On the level of numbers, \(m\) is larger than \(n\) if you can add to \(n\)to get \(m\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Definition 1.6.2. Order on natural numbers.

    Suppose \(m\) and \(n\) are natural numbers. Then \(m \ge n\) if there is a natural number \(k\) so that \(m = n + k\text{.}\) If \(m \ge n\) but \(m \ne n\) we say \(m \gt n\text{.}\) And we reverse the order of the symbols to mean the opposite direction: \(m \le n\) means \(n \ge m\) and \(m \lt n\) means \(n \gt m\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Next we define subtraction. If you have nine fruits and I take away your five guavas, how many fruits do you have in total? If \(F\) is the set of your fruits and \(G\) is the set of your guavas, what’s being asked is, what’s the cardinality of \(F \setminus G\text{?}\)
    🔗

    Definition 1.6.3. Subtraction of natural numbers.

    Suppose \(m \ge n\) are natural numbers. Let \(A\) be a set with cardinality \(m\) and let \(B \subseteq A\) be a subset of cardinality \(n\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{equation*} m - n = \card{A \setminus B}. \end{equation*}
    When we need names for the different parts, \(m - n\) is the difference, the number \(m\) being subtracted from is the minuend, and the number \(n\) being subtracted is the subtrahend.
    🔗
    🔗
    An alternative way you can think about subtraction is as the opposite of addition. Namely, \(m - n = k\) is true if and only \(n + k = m\text{.}\) For example, \(17 - 4 = 13\) because \(4 + 13 = 17\text{.}\) In words, to solve \(17 - 4\) you ask, what do I add to \(4\) to get a sum of \(17\text{?}\)
    🔗
    This perspective on subtraction is fruitful as a foundation to build further understanding, as we will see in Chapter 4. In general in mathematics, having multiple perspectives on the same idea is great. It gives us more tools and more approaches to understand things and to solve problems.
    🔗
    Note that, if we are only dealing with natural numbers, subtraction only makes sense when the subtrahend is \(\le\) the minuend. If you have \(5\) fruits you can’t take \(7\) away! In Chapter 4 we will see how to expand to a larger number system which allows you to subtract any two numbers without worrying about which one is bigger.
    🔗
    Multiplication is a step trickier to define. To illustrate, suppose you have four boxes, each of which contains six papayas. How many papayas do you have in total? One way to think of this is, how many ways are there for you to pick out a single papaya? It’s a two-step process: first pick a box, then choose a papaya from that box. Mathematically, we represent a two-step process as an ordered pair. If we number the boxes and number the papayas in each box, each choice can be represented as the pair \((b,p)\) of the box number \(b\) followed by the papaya number \(p\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Definition 1.6.4. Multiplication of natural numbers.

    Suppose \(m\) and \(n\) are natural numbers. Let \(A\) be a set of cardinality \(m\) and \(B\) be a set of cardinality \(n\text{.}\) Then
    \begin{equation*} m \cdot n = \card{A \times B}. \end{equation*}
    When we need a name for the parts of multiplication, \(m \cdot n\) is the product while \(m\) and \(n\) are the multiplicands.
    🔗
    When used with variables, multiplication is often written just by putting them next to each other, e.g. \(mn\) instead of \(m \cdot n\text{.}\) One also sees this when one of the two multiplicands is a number, such as \(3x\text{.}\) More rarely, one sees multiplication written as \(m \times n\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗
    Like with addition, you can phrase this in terms of counting choices. If you make one choice from \(m\) options then you independently make a second choice from \(n\) options, there are \(mn\) different ways of making the two-step choice. For example, if a fast food restaurant offers \(6\) sandwiches and \(4\) sides, then there are \(6 \cdot 4\) many combos you could order.
    🔗
    Now that we’ve established the meaning of the basic operations, let’s make clear the basic rules they satisfy.
    🔗

    Theorem 1.6.5. Arithmetic properties of natural numbers.

    All of the following are true for any integers \(x,y,z\text{.}\)
    Additive identity
    🔗
    \(\displaystyle x + 0 = x\)
    🔗
    Associativity of addition
    🔗
    \(\displaystyle x + (y + z) = (x + y) + z\)
    🔗
    Commutativity of addition
    🔗
    \(\displaystyle x + y = y + x\)
    🔗
    Multiplicative identity
    🔗
    \(\displaystyle x \cdot 1 = x\)
    🔗
    Multiplicative annihilation
    🔗
    \(\displaystyle x \cdot 0 = 0\)
    🔗
    Associativity of multiplication
    🔗
    \(\displaystyle x \cdot (y \cdot z) = (x \cdot y) \cdot z\)
    🔗
    Commutativity of multiplication
    🔗
    \(\displaystyle x \cdot y = y \cdot x\)
    🔗
    Distributivity
    🔗
    \(\displaystyle x \cdot (y + z) = xy + xz\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Mathematicians call these properties the commutative semi-ring axioms, but that term will not be helpful to us.
    🔗
    We will tackle exponentiation in a different way. With addition, subtraction, and multiplication, we defined these in terms of cardinalities of sets based on certain operations. That approach is possible for exponentiation—indeed, the exercises in this chapter guide you through that approach—but it is helpful to take another perspective.
    🔗
    Namely, we will consider exponentiation as iterated multiplication. In plain terms, exponentiation means repeatedly multiplying a number by itself.
    🔗

    Definition 1.6.6. Exponentiation of natural numbers.

    Let \(a\) and \(n\) be natural numbers. Then,
    \begin{equation*} a^n = \underbrace{a \cdot a \cdot \cdots \cdot a}_{n \text{ many}} \end{equation*}
    We call \(a\) the base and \(n\) the exponent.
    🔗
    🔗
    This rule doesn’t tell us what to do with an exponent of \(0\text{.}\) We will do the only sensible thing, and say that \(a^0 = 1\text{,}\) no matter what \(a\) is.
    🔗
    Like with the other operations, we can write down rules that describe how exponentiation behaves.
    🔗

    Definition 1.6.7. Rules for exponentiation.

    All of the following are true for any integers \(a,b,m,n\text{.}\)
    • \(\displaystyle a^n \cdot a^m = a^{n + m}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • \(\displaystyle (a^n)^m = a^{nm}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • \(\displaystyle a^n \cdot b^n = (ab)^n\)
      🔗
      🔗
    🔗
