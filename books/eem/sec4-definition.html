    Section 4.2 Defining the integers

    No doubt you know the solution already—throw in negative numbers!—but let’s pretend that we don’t already know about negative numbers. How might we come to invent them?
    🔗
    Our original definition of subtraction in \(\Nbb\text{,}\) namely counting what happens when you take away from a collection, won’t work. So we need a different way of thinking about subtraction to guide us. Another way we’ve understood subtraction is, it is the opposite of addition. For example, the formula \(8 - 3\) can be understood as asking, what do you need to add to \(3\) to get a sum of \(8\text{?}\) This way of thinking can be generalized. As good starting place is subtracting from \(0\text{,}\) the additive identity. The equation \(x = 0 - 4\) means, \(x\) is the value you add to \(4\) to get \(0\text{.}\) This is saying that \(x\) is a sort of opposite of \(4\text{.}\) It turns out that this one idea is enough to completely solve our subtraction problem.
    🔗

    Definition 4.2.1. Additive inverses.

    If \(x\) is a number then the additive inverse of \(x\text{,}\) written \(-x\text{,}\) is the number so that
    \begin{equation*} x + (-x) = 0. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗
    Inverse means opposite. So additive inverse means opposite in the sense of addition. The following theorem makes precise the idea that subtraction is the opposite of addition.
    🔗

    Theorem 4.2.2.

    For any two numbers \(x\) and \(y\text{,}\) subtracting is the same as adding an inverse:
    \begin{equation*} x - y = x + (-y). \end{equation*}
    Thus, for any number \(x\) we have
    \begin{equation*} 0 - x = -x. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 4.2.3. Positive and negative numbers.

    A positive number is one which is \(\gt 0\text{.}\) A negative number is an additive inverse of a positive number. The number \(0\) is considered neither positive nor negative. The sign of a number refers to whether it is positive or negative.
    🔗
    🔗

    Definition 4.2.4. Absolute value.

    The absolute value, also called magnitude and written \(\abs x\text{,}\) of a number \(x\) is its distance from \(0\text{.}\) More precisely, if \(x \ge 0\) then \(\abs x = x\) and if \(x\) is negative then \(\abs x = -x\text{.}\)
    🔗
    🔗

    Aside
    Warning! While we write negative numbers by putting a minus sign in front, such as \(-8\text{,}\) if you put a minus sign in front of a variable the result could be either negative or positive. For example, \(-x\) will be negative if \(x\) is positive but it will be positive if \(x\) is negative. In general you won’t know which.
    🔗

    Definition 4.2.5. The integers \(\Zbb\).

    The integers consist of the natural numbers plus including additive inverses for every natural number. That is, the integers consist of both the positive and negative whole numbers. We write \(\Zbb\) for the set of integers. (‘Z’ stands for the German word Zahlen, meaning integers. Mathematics is a cosmopolitan discipline so some symbols come from non-English words.)
    🔗
    🔗
    Note that while we have now said what the integers are, we haven’t yet said how to make sense of arithmetic with integers. It will turn out that all of your favorite arithmetic properties about the natural numbers will continue to be true about the integers, plus the new property that additive inverses always exist. For convenience they are listed below, and afterward we will talk about how to make sense of arithmetic in \(\Zbb\text{.}\)
    🔗

    Theorem 4.2.6. Arithmetic properties of integers.

    All of the following are true for any integers \(x,y,z\text{.}\)
    Additive identity
    🔗
    \(\displaystyle x + 0 = x\)
    🔗
    Additive inverses
    🔗
    \(x\) has an additive inverse.
    🔗
    Associativity of addition
    🔗
    \(\displaystyle x + (y + z) = (x + y) + z\)
    🔗
    Commutativity of addition
    🔗
    \(\displaystyle x + y = y + x\)
    🔗
    Multiplicative identity
    🔗
    \(\displaystyle x \cdot 1 = x\)
    🔗
    Multiplicative annihilation
    🔗
    \(\displaystyle x \cdot 0 = 0\)
    🔗
    Associativity of multiplication
    🔗
    \(\displaystyle x \cdot (y \cdot z) = (x \cdot y) \cdot z\)
    🔗
    Commutativity of multiplication
    🔗
    \(\displaystyle x \cdot y = y \cdot x\)
    🔗
    Distributivity
    🔗
    \(\displaystyle x \cdot (y + z) = xy + xz\)
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Mathematicians call these properties the commutative ring axioms. There are many interesting mathematical structures, called commutative rings, which have these same properties. We will see one example later in this chapter when we look at clocks.
