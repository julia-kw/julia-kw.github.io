    Section 6.6 Exercises

    Exercises Exercises

    1.

    Calculate the following sum and difference. Show all steps in your calculation.
    \begin{equation*} 13.4 + 29.9, \qquad 4.1043 - 3.255 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    2.

    Each year, an investment increases in value from its current value by \(5\%\text{.}\) If it is initially worth \(1000\) dollars, how much is it worth after two years? After six years? After twelve years? Round all your answers to the nearest hundreth.
    🔗
    Hint 1.
    You can write \(5\%\) in decimal as \(0.05\) and \(105\%\) is \(1.05\text{.}\) Using this, write a formula which describes the value of the investment after \(n\) years.
    🔗
    🔗
    Hint 2.
    If you’re completely stuck, reread the section from about percents.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    3.

    For the following products, state how many digits past the decimal point the product will have.
    \begin{equation*} 12.3 \times 123, \qquad 0.13 \times 0.43, \qquad 1.0001 \times 1.0000001, \qquad 123 \times 0.00123 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    4.

    Calculate the product \(3.4 \cdot 4.3\) by the box method. That is, individually do the four products
    \begin{equation*} 3 \times 4, \quad 3 \times 0.3, \quad 0.4 \times 4, \quad 0.4 \times 0.3 \end{equation*}
    and add them up to get the sum. For each of the four intermediate products, how many digits past the decimal point does it have?
    🔗
    Hint.
    One way to determine the number of digits past the decimal point is to write things as fractions: \(0.3 = \frac3{10}\) and \(0.4 = \frac4{10}\text{.}\) Then use rules for multiplying fractions.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    5.

    For each of the following numbers, round to the nearest ten and the nearest tenth using each of: rounding toward zero, rounding up, rounding down, and rounding to the nearest ten/tenth. Clearly label your rounding method for each.
    \begin{equation*} -73.97, \qquad 1349.15 \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    6.

    Consider a new rule for rounding, namely rounding away from zero. That is, to round a number \(x\) away from zero to the nearest integer you move away from zero toward the next integer. Demonstrate this new rule by rounding away from zero to the next integer for all of the following.
    \begin{equation*} 14.2, \qquad -3.9, \qquad 16, \qquad -4.2 \end{equation*}
    For which numbers is this rule equivalent to rounding down? For which numbers is this rule equivalent to rounding up?
    🔗
    🔗

    7.

    Write the following numbers in scientific notation. For each one, how many significant digits does it have?
    \begin{equation*} 134.5, \qquad 0.0004130, \qquad 920100000, \qquad 723 \times 10^{-6} \end{equation*}
    🔗
    🔗

    8.

    Come up with a rule for how to multiply two numbers written in scientific notation. Demonstrate your rule by calculating the product
    \begin{equation*} 1.2 \times 10^{3} \cdot 2.0 \times 10^{5}. \end{equation*}
    🔗
    Hint.
    A product like \(1.2 \times 10^{3} \cdot 2.0 \times 10^{5}\) can be thought of as multiplying four numbers together. Use rules for multiplication and exponents to rewrite.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗
    Numbers with a repeating decimal expansion are examples of what mathematicians call geometric sums. Namely, a geometric sum is one of the form
    \begin{equation*} a + ar + ar^2 + ar^3 + \cdots + ar^n + \cdots. \end{equation*}
    The rule for geometric sums is, if \(\abs r \lt 1\) then the sum converges to \(\frac{a}{1 - r}\text{.}\) For example,
    \begin{align*} 1.\overline{1} \amp= 1 + \frac1{10} + \frac1{100} + \cdots + \frac1{10^n} + \cdots\\ \amp= \frac{1}{1 - \frac{1}{10}}\\ \amp= \frac{1}{9/10}\\ \amp= \frac{10}{9} = 1 + \frac19. \end{align*}
    In this case, we had \(a = 1\) and \(r = \frac{1}{10}\text{.}\)
    🔗

    9.

    Write the numbers \(3.\overline{3}\) and \(9.\overline{9}\) as infinite sums and use the rule for geometric sums to determine their values. What do your answers tell you about the rational numbers these are equal to?
    🔗
    Hint.
    Follow the same steps as the example.
    🔗
    🔗
    🔗

    10.

    Consider the two numbers \(x\) and \(y\) given by the following patterns for their decimal expansions. Each number has an integer part of \(0\text{.}\)
    • The digits after the decimal point for \(x\) are obtained by writing the integers \(1\) through \(9\) in order one after another, looping back to \(1\) whenever they reach \(9\text{.}\)
      🔗
      🔗
    • The digits after the decimal point for \(y\) are obtained by writing the integers starting at \(1\) in order one after another, never looping back.
      🔗
      🔗
    For each of these numbers, is it rational or irrational? Explain your answers.
    🔗
    Hint.
    If you are not sure, begin by writing out the decimal expansion of the number. What patterns can you notice?
